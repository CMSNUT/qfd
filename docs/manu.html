––– title: “Synergistic Mechanisms of Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus membranaceus Combination Against Chronic Heart Failure Due to Dilated Cardiomyopathy: Insights from Multi–Omics and Causal Inference” format: html: toc: true number–sections: true abstract: > The combination of the medicinal plants Aconitum carmichaelii (AC) and Astragalus membranaceus (AM) has been an effective centuries–old clinical regimen for dilated cardiomyopathy–induced chronic heart failure (DCM–CHF), while its synergistic mechanisms remain unclear. Here, we integrate network pharmacology, multi omics and causal inference–based bioinformatics, and molecular docking to systematically clarify the synergistic mechanisms of the AC–AM combination against DCM–CHF. Drug and disease targets were systematically identified via public database mining. In total, 24 blood–absorbable active compounds were screened with distinct targeting characteristics. AC preferentially regulates myocardial contractility and mitochondrial metabolism in cardiomyocytes and fibroblasts, whereas AM modulates inflammation and vascular protection in immune and endothelial cells. Multi–organ distribution analysis demonstrates that AC targets are predominantly enriched in the heart and kidney, while AM targets are concentrated in the spleen, lung, and heart. Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization analyses identified [X] robust causal targets (PPH4 > 0.8). Opposing normalized enrichment score patterns were observed for AC (NES = −1.52) and AM (NES = 1.83). Molecular docking confirmed stable binding of neoline and calycosin to core targets. Consistent target expression trends in external datasets validated result reliability. The results show the AC–AM combination exerts synergistic cardioprotective effects through convergent modulation of mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling. Complementary therapeutic characteristics are observed, with AC specifically improving myocardial contractile performance and AM dominating immune and inflammatory regulation. This work provides a mechanistic basis for the evidence–based clinical application of the AC–AM medicinal plant combination in the treatment of DCM–induced CHF.

Introduction

Chronic heart failure (CHF) is the terminal common pathological manifestation of various cardiovascular diseases and the leading cause of global cardiovascular mortality. The global prevalence of adult CHF continues to rise in recent years. Affected by genetic variation, environmental incentives and metabolic abnormalities, the incidence of refractory heart failure in young and middle–aged populations has increased year by year. Genetic cardiomyopathy is the primary cause of heart failure, malignant arrhythmia and sudden cardiac death in young and middle–aged individuals. Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), the most common hereditary primary cardiomyopathy, is pathologically characterized by progressive biventricular dilation, ventricular wall thinning, and persistent irreversible decline in myocardial systolic function. Its pathological process involves cascading injuries including cardiomyocyte apoptosis, interstitial fibrosis, immune inflammatory infiltration, and energy metabolism exhaustion. At present, standardized comprehensive intervention systems for DCM–CHF have been established clinically, mainly including neuroendocrine inhibitor drugs such as β–blockers, angiotensin–converting enzyme inhibitors/angiotensin receptor blockers, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, as well as invasive treatments such as ventricular resynchronization device–assisted circulation and end–stage heart transplantation. These interventions can effectively antagonize excessive neuroendocrine activation, improve short–term hemodynamic indicators, relieve clinical symptoms such as chest tightness and fatigue, and reduce the risk of acute malignant cardiovascular events. Despite these pharmacological advances, substantial unmet needs persist, as many patients continue to experience progressive decline in cardiac function, and the underlying pathological mechanisms—encompassing myocardial energy metabolic reprogramming, mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic low–grade inflammation, and maladaptive fibrotic remodeling—remain incompletely addressed by conventional therapeutic approaches.

Medicinal plants have been used for centuries as adjunctive therapies for heart failure across various traditional medical systems worldwide. The empirical evidence supporting their clinical benefits in alleviating symptoms, improving functional capacity, and enhancing quality of life in heart failure patients has long been recognized. In recent decades, substantial progress has been made in elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying these therapeutic effects. Preclinical investigations have identified multiple bioactive compounds that target pathways central to heart failure pathogenesis—including AMPK–mediated energy sensing, PPAR–driven metabolic regulation, NF–κB–dependent inflammatory cascades, and TGF–β/Smad fibrotic signaling1. These mechanistic studies have revealed that medicinal plants typically exert polypharmacological actions through multi–target interactions, simultaneously influencing diverse biological processes, a feature that distinguishes them from single–molecule synthetic drugs and aligns well with the complex, multi–system nature of CHF.

Medicinal plants, such as Astragalus membranaceus, Panax ginseng, Aconitum carmichaelii, Salvia miltiorrhiza, Crataegus pinnatifida, Ginkgo biloba and Descurainia sophia, have been used for centuries as adjunctive therapies for heart failure across various traditional medical systems worldwide, and the empirical evidence supporting their clinical benefits in alleviating symptoms, improving functional capacity, and enhancing quality of life in heart failure patients has long been recognized; abundant in structurally diverse and functionally specific natural small molecular compounds, such botanical resources can synergistically regulate complex pathological networks of diseases via multi–component, multi–target and multi–pathway modes, boasting prominent strengths including high safety, low side effects and stable long–term intervention efficacy, and have thus evolved into core materials for adjuvant management of chronic complicated illnesses and the discovery of novel lead compounds. In recent decades, remarkable advances have been achieved in decoding the molecular mechanisms behind their cardioprotective effects, with extensive preclinical research pinpointing a wide array of bioactive ingredients that modulate core signaling cascades governing heart failure pathogenesis—such as AMPK–mediated energy sensing, PPAR–governed metabolic control, NF–κB–dependent inflammatory responses, and TGF–β/Smad fibrotic signaling; relevant mechanistic explorations further demonstrate that medicinal plants generally produce polypharmacological effects through simultaneous interactions with multiple molecular targets to modulate disparate biological processes, a characteristic that differentiates them from single–agent synthetic pharmaceuticals and perfectly matches the complicated multi–system pathological features of CHF. Meanwhile, a large body of in vitro and in vivo pharmacological assays as well as clinical adjuvant trials conducted in recent years have validated that the active components derived from medicinal plants ameliorate ventricular remodeling and retard heart failure deterioration by modulating myocardial energy metabolism, suppressing oxidative damage, counteracting myocardial fibrosis, balancing the cardiac immune microenvironment, and maintaining calcium signal homeostasis, endowing them with distinct superiority in long–term adjunctive intervention for DCM–CHF.

Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus membranaceus are the most classic and widely applied medicinal plants combination in the field of cardiovascular protection, with definite clinical efficacy in adjuvant treatment of heart failure. Modern pharmacological studies have verified that diterpenoid alkaloids from Aconitum carmichaelii possess significant cardiotonic, positive inotropic, calcium channel regulatory and anti–inflammatory activities, which can directly act on myocardial ion channels to enhance myocardial contractility, improve ventricular diastolic function and inhibit acute myocardial inflammatory injury. Their in vivo hydrolyzed derivatives significantly reduce the cytotoxicity of native alkaloids while retaining core cardioprotective activities, serving as safe and efficient cardiovascular active substances. Astragalus membranaceus is rich in isoflavones and polysaccharides. Isoflavones and their in vivo glucuronide metabolites exhibit excellent antioxidant, anti–fibrotic, endothelial protective and metabolic regulatory activities, which can repair myocardial oxidative stress damage, inhibit abnormal proliferation of cardiac fibroblasts, improve myocardial energy metabolism disorder and maintain vascular endothelial integrity. Notably, these two plants have been shown to act through distinct yet potentially complementary biological pathways, with Aconitum primarily influencing cardiac contractile function and energy metabolism, and Astragalus predominantly modulating inflammatory responses and vascular homeostasis. However, significant gaps remain in understanding the comprehensive mechanisms by which this classic herb pair—Aconitum carmichaelii (Fuzi) and Astragalus membranaceus (Huangqi)—combats dilated cardiomyopathy‑related chronic heart failure (DCM–CHF). First, the synergistic active ingredients and in vivo metabolic profiles of this botanical combination have not been systematically characterized, hindering a thorough elucidation of its pharmacodynamic material basis. Second, the two herbs exhibit marked differences in molecular targets, tissue distribution, and cell‑type‑specific regulatory properties, adding complexity to the mechanistic basis of their combined action. Third, current pharmacological studies are largely confined to phenotypic observations, making it difficult to distinguish their independent functional modules from their interactive synergistic pathways. Critically, the absence of genetic causal evidence further constrains the modern clinical application and translational development of this classic herb pair. Overall, existing research has yet to systematically unravel the multi‑layered, multi‑scale molecular, cellular, and systemic synergistic mechanisms that underlie the holistic therapeutic efficacy of this combined regimen.

Recent methodological progress in systems pharmacology and multi–omics integration has created unprecedented opportunities to unravel the intricate therapeutic mechanisms of medicinal plants. Network pharmacology has emerged as a powerful tool to predict compound–target interactions and build pharmacological networks that recapitulate the polypharmacological traits of medicinal plants. The rapid development of bioinformatics and genetic epidemiology has provided innovative tools for investigating the mechanisms of complex medicinal plant combinations. Genomics and proteomics profiling further support systematic characterization of the multi–organ distribution patterns of drug targets, mapping the abundance and activation status of target proteins and their encoding genes across cardiac, hepatic, renal and other peripheral tissues, which clarifies tissue–specific pharmacological responsiveness and explains the systemic regulatory characteristics of multi–component herbal formulas. Bulk transcriptomics enables panoramic profiling of dysregulated gene expression in diseased myocardium, whereas single–cell transcriptomics resolves cell–type–specific transcriptional signatures within cardiac tissue, delineating the cellular localization and functional specialization of therapeutic targets. Genome–wide association study (GWAS) datasets identify genetic susceptibility loci for heart diseases, while expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) datasets link genetic variants to aberrant tissue gene expression, enabling deep mechanistic exploration bridging molecular phenotypes and genetic determinants. Built upon these multi–omics resources, causal inference frameworks integrating Mendelian randomization (MR) and Bayesian colocalization leverage genetic variants as instrumental variables to eliminate confounding factors and reverse causality inherent to conventional observational studies. This framework precisely pinpoints core causal targets governing disease progression and substantially elevates the reliability and rigor of investigations into herbal active components and their mechanisms. Collectively, these integrated approaches, merging computational predictions with multi–layered omics data and causal inference, can reveal not only the molecular targets of individual compounds and their distribution signatures across diverse tissues, cell subtypes and signaling pathways but also the synergistic interactions among components that underlie the enhanced efficacy of herbal combinations, thus establishing a novel technical paradigm for modern research on medicinal plant combinations.

In this study, we aim to systematically elucidate the synergistic molecular mechanisms of the combination of Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus membranaceus intervenes in DCM‑CHF, from blood‑absorbed bioactive components, multi‑tissue target distribution, genetic causal mechanisms, cell‑type‑specific regulation, and pathway‑level synergistic modulation. Specifically, we first identified the blood absorbed active ingredients of the combination from public databases and literatures, and predicted their potential targets based public online platforms. Subsequently, we constructed a comprehensive disease related gene set by integrating three sources: (i) DCM HF genetic risk genes derived from GWAS, (ii) high confidence DCM CHF associated genes reported in the literatures, and (iii) transcriptome derived genes, including differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and module genes identified through weighted gene co expression network analysis (WGCNA). We further obtained overlapping genes between the screened drug targets and the integrated disease gene set and performed multi–organ target localization and functional enrichment analyses to systematically characterize the tissue distribution and biological functions of these intersecting target genes. Furthermore, we applied Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization to establish genetic causal links between the core targets and DCM, while integrating single cell RNA sequencing data to finely map the cell type specific expression profiles of the targets and using single sample gene set enrichment analysis (ssGSEA) to quantify inter individual variations in pathway activity. Finally, molecular docking was employed to validate the binding affinities between key bioactive compounds and their target proteins, and the expression trends of the core targets were verified in an independent external cohort. This study, for the first time, identifies the key targets, core regulatory pathways, cell type specific features, and multi organ synergistic patterns of the Aconitum–Astragalus combination against DCM CHF, thereby laying a theoretical basis for its prospective clinical translation in the prevention and treatment of the disease, as well as offering guidance for subsequent mechanistic experiments.

Materials and Methods

Blood–absorbed bioactive compounds retrieval and targets prediction

Blood–absorbed bioactive compounds of Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus mongholicus were retrieved from the Database of Constituents Absorbed into the Blood and Metabolites of Traditional Chinese Medicine (DCABM–TCM, http://bionet.ncpsb.org.cn/dcabm–tcm/)1, where the two medicinal materials were searched by inputting their Chinese pinyin “FU ZI” and “HUANG QI” under the “Herb” category (database access: November 15, 2025). In addition, blood–absorbed constituents of the two medicinal plants identified via HPLC–MS/MS were additionally collected from published peer–reviewed papers. Subsequently, all compound information obtained from DCABM–TCM and supplementary literature was merged, followed by duplicate removal to form a compounds dataset.

Potential targets of the screened medicinal plant–derived compounds were predicted using four authoritative target prediction platforms, including SwissTargetPrediction (https://www.swisstargetprediction.ch/)2–4, PharmMapper (https://www.lilab–ecust.cn/pharmmapper/)5–7, SEA (https://seadev.docking.org/)8 and BATMAN–TCM 2.0 (http://bionet.ncpsb.org.cn/batman–tcm/#/home)9, with respective screening thresholds: confidence score ≥ 0.7 for SwissTargetPrediction, normalized fit score ≥ 0.7 for PharmMapper, probability score ≥ 0.7 for SEA and confidence score ≥ 0.84 for BATMAN–TCM 2.0. All retrieved gene symbols were standardized to HGNC official nomenclature, and duplicate entries were eliminated to establish a high–confidence target dataset of medicinal plant–derived compounds.

DCM–CHF–associated genes identification

A high–confidence, comprehensive target panel underlying DCM–CHF was established through multi–omics integration and literature mining, encompassing bulk myocardial transcriptomic profiles, genome–wide association study (GWAS) data, and published disease–associated literature.

The gene expression dataset GSE57338 (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=57338) was retrieved from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database, from which we exclusively selected left ventricular myocardial biopsies from 136 healthy controls and 82 patients with DCM–CHF. All raw data underwent standard preprocessing pipelines, followed by probe–level filtering to retain only stably detectable transcripts. Differential expression analysis was subsequently performed using the R package limma10. Genes with |log₂(fold change)| > 0.585 and adjusted FDR < 0.05 were identified as differentially expressed genes (DEGs).

Weighted gene co–expression network analysis (WGCNA) was conducted on the filtered transcriptomic dataset using the R WGCNA package11 to screen DCM–CHF–related gene co–expression modules. Hierarchical clustering was applied to eliminate outlier samples. An unsigned topological overlap matrix was built based on pairwise Pearson correlations, and the soft–thresholding power (β) was determined using the pickSoftThreshold function to achieve scale–free network topology approximation. Gene modules were identified by average–linkage hierarchical clustering and dynamic tree–cutting, with a minimum module size of 30 genes and a module merging threshold of 0.25. Modules with module–trait correlation |r| > 0.6 and P < 0.01 for DCM–CHF clinical traits were defined as phenotype–specific key modules. DCM–CHF–related genes in key modules were further screened under the thresholds of module membership (|MM|) > 0.6 and gene significance (|GS|) > 0.2.

GWAS summary statistics of DCM genetic risk loci were retrieved from the HERMES Consortium European-ancestry meta-analysis (dataset: HERMES2_GWAS_DCM_EUR; download link: https://cvd.hugeamp.org/dinspector.html?dataset=Henry2024_HFsubtypes_EU; download date: November 25, 2025), which included 6,001 DCM cases and 449,382 controls12. Genome-wide significant variants ((P < 5 ^{-8})) were mapped to risk genes positional mapping within ±50 kb of lead variants, supplemented by cardiac tissue eQTL colocalization evidence.

In addition, additional DCM-CHF-associated genes were supplemented through manual curation of authoritative literature. Finally, redundant genes sourced from transcriptomic profiling, GWAS analysis and literature mining were removed, yielding a finalized integrated DCM–CHF–associated genes.

2.3 Protein-protein interaction network construction and hub genes identification

Candidate therapeutic targets were obtained by intersecting drug targets of medicinal plants and DCM-CHF disease-related targets. The overlapping target genes were submitted to the STRING v12.0 database (https://string-db.org/)13 to construct a human protein–protein interaction (PPI) network, with species restricted to Homo sapiens and a confidence score threshold of ≥ 0.7. The resulting PPI network was visualized using Cytoscape v3.10.214, and all isolated nodes were removed to eliminate confounding factors and ensure the reliability of subsequent topological analyses.

Functional module clustering of the refined PPI network was performed using the Molecular Complex Detection (MCODE) algorithm15, with parameters configured as degree cutoff = 2 and k-core = 2. Maximal Clique Centrality (MCC), Maximum Neighborhood Component (MNC), and Degree, three topological metrics integrated within the cytoHubba16 plugin of Cytoscape14, were applied for hub gene ranking. Genes ranked within the top 20 by each of the three metrics were intersected and merged with MCODE-derived module genes. The merged gene set was defined as hub genes for subsequent analysis.

Multi–organ expression profiling of hub genes

Tissue expression profiles of the hub genes across cardiac, renal, lung, splenic, and hepatic tissues were annotated using the BioGPS database (http://biogps.org/)17–19 and the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) (https://www.proteinatlas.org/)20,21. The GeneAtlas U133A (gcrma) dataset (available: http://biogps.org/dataset/GSE1133/geneatlas-u133a-gcrma/), which profiles 79 human tissue samples using Affymetrix U133A arrays with gcRMA normalization, was utilized for human tissue expression analysis. For HPA-based protein-level profiling, protein expression data were retrieved and processed using the R package HPAanalyze (v1.30.0)22. Transcriptomics data within HPA were derived from RNA deep-sequencing (RNA-seq) across normal tissue types. Quantitative tissue expression heatmaps were subsequently generated to compare the tissue enrichment characteristics of these hub genes.

Functional enrichment analysis

Gene Ontology functional enrichment (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analyses were performed using the R package clusterProfiler23. The enrichment thresholds were set at P < 0.05 and false discovery rate (FDR) < 0.01.

Myocardial tissue immune infiltration analysis

Transcriptomic profiles of dilated cardiomyopathy with chronic heart failure (DCM-CHF) and normal myocardial samples were analyzed using the R package IOBR (v 2.2.3)24, which integrates multiple state-of-the-art algorithms for tumor microenvironment deconvolution. Two complementary deconvolution methods, CIBERSORT25 and MCP-counter26, were used to evaluate immune cell infiltration. For CIBERSORT analysis, 1,000 permutations were performed, and only samples with a deconvolution P value < 0.05 were retained for subsequent analyses to ensure reliable estimation. Differential immune infiltration between the disease and control groups was assessed using two-tailed Student’s t-tests. Additionally, Pearson correlation analysis was performed to evaluate the associations between the expression levels of core hub genes and the infiltration abundances of critical pathogenic immune cell populations.

Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization

To screen for genes causally affecting dilated cardiomyopathy‑related heart failure (DCM‑CHF), two‑sample Mendelian randomization (MR)27 was performed by integrating HERMES 2.0 GWAS summary statistics with GTEx v8 (https://gtexportal.org/) heart left ventricular myocardial eQTL data (download date: November 25, 2025). Instrumental variables (IV) were selected at P < 5 × 10^−8, pruned for linkage disequilibrium (LD) (r^2 <0.001 within a 1–Mb window), filtered by F–statistic > 10, and excluded from the MHC region. The inverse–variance weighted (IVW)28 method with multiplicative random effects was employed as the primary analysis, complemented by MR–Egger29 and weighted median30 estimators. Horizontal pleiotropy was assessed via the MR–Egger intercept, heterogeneity via Cochran’s Q, and robustness via leave–one–out and funnel plot inspections.

Bayesian colocalization31 was subsequently performed using default priors, and a PPH₄ > 0.8 threshold was adopted to identify high confidence shared causal variants, effectively mitigating reverse causality and confounding bias.
