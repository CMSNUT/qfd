Abstract

Medicinal plants have long been used as adjunctive therapies for chronic heart failure (CHF) due to dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), yet their synergistic mechanisms remain poorly understood. This study integrates network pharmacology, bulk transcriptomics (GSE57338), single-cell RNA sequencing (GSE183852), WGCNA, Mendelian randomization with Bayesian colocalization (GTEx v8 eQTL, HERMES2 GWAS), molecular docking, ssGSEA, and external validation (GSE5406, GSE116250) to elucidate the synergistic mechanisms of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination. Twenty-four blood-absorbed active components were identified. Targets stratified into Aconitum- and Astragalus-associated categories with distinct functional enrichments—Aconitum targets in contractility, ion channels, and mitochondrial metabolism; Astragalus targets in immune modulation, inflammation, and vascular protection. Multi-organ distribution showed Aconitum targets enriched in heart and kidney, Astragalus targets in spleen, lung, and heart. MR identified causal targets with PPH4 > 0.85. Single-cell analysis revealed differential cellular preferences: Aconitum in cardiomyocytes and fibroblasts; Astragalus in immune and endothelial cells. Molecular docking validated neoline and calycosin binding (−8.2 and −7.6 kcal/mol). ssGSEA showed opposing patterns (Aconitum: NES = −1.52; Astragalus: NES = 1.83). External validation confirmed robustness. The combination exerts synergistic effects through convergence on mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling, complemented by Aconitum-specific actions on contractility and Astragalus-specific actions on immune modulation, providing a mechanistic foundation for evidence-based application.

1 Introduction

Chronic heart failure (CHF) affects approximately 64 million people worldwide and remains a leading cause of cardiovascular morbidity, mortality, and healthcare expenditure1. Among its diverse etiologies, dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) ranks as the third most common cause, characterized by progressive left ventricular dilation and systolic dysfunction that frequently progresses to end-stage heart failure requiring advanced interventions such as cardiac transplantation or mechanical circulatory support. DCM is particularly devastating in younger populations, where it constitutes a major cause of sudden cardiac death and heart failure-related disability. The clinical management of DCM-related CHF currently relies on guideline-directed medical therapy, including β-blockers, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, which primarily modulate neurohormonal pathways to alleviate symptoms and slow disease progression3. Despite these pharmacological advances, substantial unmet needs persist, as many patients continue to experience progressive decline in cardiac function, and the underlying pathological mechanisms—encompassing myocardial energy metabolic reprogramming, mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic low-grade inflammation, and maladaptive fibrotic remodeling—remain incompletely addressed by conventional therapeutic approaches.

Medicinal plants have been used for centuries as adjunctive therapies for heart failure across various traditional medical systems worldwide. The empirical evidence supporting their clinical benefits in alleviating symptoms, improving functional capacity, and enhancing quality of life in heart failure patients has long been recognized. In recent decades, substantial progress has been made in elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying these therapeutic effects. Preclinical investigations have identified multiple bioactive compounds that target pathways central to heart failure pathogenesis—including AMPK-mediated energy sensing, PPAR-driven metabolic regulation, NF-κB-dependent inflammatory cascades, and TGF-β/Smad fibrotic signaling1. These mechanistic studies have revealed that medicinal plants typically exert polypharmacological actions through multi-target interactions, simultaneously influencing diverse biological processes, a feature that distinguishes them from single-molecule synthetic drugs and aligns well with the complex, multi-system nature of CHF.

Among the various plant-based therapeutics employed for heart conditions, the combination of Aconitum carmichaelii (commonly known as Fuzi in traditional practice) and Astragalus membranaceus (Huangqi) occupies a particularly prominent position in the traditional medicinal systems of East Asia, where it has been used for centuries to address symptoms associated with cardiac insufficiency1. The pairing of these two plants appears in numerous classic formulations for cardiovascular support, including QiFu Decoction, ShenFu Injection, and various compound preparations used in hospital-based traditional medicine settings for acute and chronic heart failure management. Experimental studies have demonstrated that Aconitum alkaloids—including neoline, hypaconine, mesaconine, and songorine—exert positive inotropic effects, enhance mitochondrial respiratory function, and modulate ion channel activity, while Astragalus isoflavonoids and polysaccharides exhibit anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, and endothelial-protective properties4. Notably, these two plants have been shown to act through distinct yet potentially complementary biological pathways, with Aconitum primarily influencing cardiac contractile function and energy metabolism, and Astragalus predominantly modulating inflammatory responses and vascular homeostasis. However, the integrated mechanistic basis for their combined therapeutic efficacy—specifically how these two plants synergistically interact at the molecular, cellular, and systemic levels to combat DCM-related CHF—has remained largely unexplored, with existing investigations limited to single-component or single-pathway analyses.

Recent methodological advances in systems pharmacology and multi-omics integration have opened new avenues for deciphering the complex mechanisms of plant-based therapeutics. Network pharmacology approaches have proven valuable for predicting compound-target interactions and constructing pharmacological networks that capture the polypharmacological nature of medicinal plants7. More importantly, the integration of transcriptomics with network pharmacology enables the identification of disease-relevant target modules and the prioritization of candidate active compounds based on their ability to modulate pathologically perturbed pathways. Furthermore, the emergence of single-cell RNA sequencing has provided unprecedented resolution for dissecting cell-type-specific drug actions, revealing that the failing heart comprises distinct cellular compartments—cardiomyocytes, fibroblasts, immune cells, and endothelial cells—each contributing uniquely to disease pathogenesis and offering cell-specific therapeutic opportunities8. The incorporation of human genetic evidence through Mendelian randomization (MR) and Bayesian colocalization represents a further paradigm shift, allowing investigators to establish causal relationships between molecular targets and disease susceptibility, thereby prioritizing biologically validated targets for therapeutic intervention9. These integrated approaches, combining computational prediction with multi-layered omics data and causal inference, can reveal not only the molecular targets of individual compounds but also the synergistic interactions between components that underlie the enhanced efficacy of plant combinations.

Against this backdrop, the present study was undertaken to systematically elucidate the synergistic mechanisms of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination against DCM-related CHF. We established an integrative multi-omics and causal inference framework that combines network pharmacology (SwissTargetPrediction, PharmMapper, SEA), bulk transcriptomics (GSE57338), single-cell RNA sequencing (GSE183852), WGCNA, multi-organ-target analysis (BioGPS and HPA), MR with Bayesian colocalization (GTEx v8 eQTL and HERMES2 DCM GWAS), immune infiltration analysis, molecular docking, and ssGSEA. Using 24 blood-absorbed active components as entry points, we aimed to: (1) define the intersection network between predicted targets and DCM-associated genes; (2) stratify targets by plant origin and map their functional enrichment and multi-organ distribution patterns; (3) establish causal relationships between core targets and DCM through MR and colocalization; (4) validate physical binding interactions via molecular docking; (5) characterize cell-type-specific expression patterns at single-cell resolution; (6) quantify pathway activity differences at the individual-sample level through ssGSEA; and (7) validate findings in independent external cohorts. Ultimately, this comprehensive analysis aims to construct a multi-layered “component–target–cell–organ–pathway” synergistic network that provides mechanistic insights into how the two plants cooperate to combat DCM-related CHF.

2 Materials and Methods

2.1 Active Components and Target Prediction

The blood-absorbed active components of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination were derived from published HPLC-TOF-MS data with SUS-plot identification1. A total of 24 components were included, comprising Aconitum-derived diterpenoid alkaloids (n=18) and Astragalus-derived isoflavonoids and metabolites (n=6) (Table 1). The Aconitum alkaloids included karakolidine, senbusine B, songorine, talatizamine, chasmanine, mesaconine, hypaconine, dehydration isotalatizidine, demethyl karakolidine, demethyl isotalatizidine, demethyl dehydrogen isotalatizidine, demethyl neoline, chuanfunine, 16-β-hydroxycardiopetaline, dehydration karakoline, demethyl dehydrogen neoline, fuziline, neoline, and 14-acetyltalatizamine. The Astragalus components included calycosin-O-glucuronide, formononetin-O-glucuronide, calycosin, and formononetin. Neoline and calycosin have been previously validated as core quality markers1. Targets for each component were predicted using three complementary computational approaches: SwissTargetPrediction5, PharmMapper6, and Similarity Ensemble Approach (SEA)7. Predicted targets above respective confidence thresholds were retained and standardized to official gene symbols using the UniProt database. Only targets predicted by at least two methods or with high confidence scores were included in subsequent analyses.

2.1.1 2.2 Bulk Transcriptomic Data Processing and Differential Expression Analysis

The bulk transcriptomic dataset GSE57338 was obtained from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database, comprising 136 healthy control samples and 82 DCM-HF samples from left ventricular tissue. Raw expression data were normalized using the Robust Multi-array Average (RMA) method. Differential expression analysis between DCM-HF and healthy controls was performed using the limma R package (v3.56.2)10, with thresholds of |log₂ fold change| > 1 and adjusted p-value < 0.05. Principal component analysis was performed to visualize the overall transcriptomic differences between groups. Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses were conducted on the differentially expressed genes to identify the key biological processes and pathways altered in DCM.

2.2 Weighted Gene Co-Expression Network Analysis (WGCNA)

WGCNA was performed using the WGCNA R package (v1.72-5)11 to identify co-expression modules associated with DCM status. A signed hybrid network was constructed using the blockwiseModules function, with soft-thresholding power selected based on the scale-free topology criterion. Module eigengenes were calculated for each module, and module-trait relationships were assessed by correlating module eigengenes with DCM status. Hub genes within disease-associated modules were identified based on module membership (MM) and gene significance (GS) criteria, with thresholds of MM > 0.8 and GS > 0.2. The top hub genes were further analyzed for their functional annotations and biological relevance.

2.3 Disease Target Integration

DCM disease targets were compiled from four complementary sources to ensure comprehensive coverage: (1) Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) : genes significantly altered between DCM and healthy controls from GSE57338; (2) WGCNA hub genes: hub genes from DCM-associated co-expression modules; (3) GWAS genetic targets: genes at loci identified by the HERMES2 GWAS for DCM12; and (4) Literature-curated targets: high-evidence DCM-associated genes from published literature2. Only genes supported by at least two sources were considered high-confidence DCM-associated genes for subsequent integration.

2.4 Multi-Organ-Target Analysis

Tissue-specific expression patterns of the predicted targets were analyzed using BioGPS13 and the Human Protein Atlas (HPA)14. The expression enrichment scores for each target gene across heart, kidney, spleen, lung, and liver tissues were extracted and normalized. Genes were categorized based on their preferential tissue expression, defined as having at least 2-fold higher expression in a particular tissue compared to the average expression across all tissues. These tissue distribution patterns were then mapped to the traditional meridian affiliations to examine the molecular basis of this concept.

2.5 Mendelian Randomization and Bayesian Colocalization

Two-sample Mendelian randomization was performed using the TwoSampleMR R package (v0.5.8)15 to evaluate causal relationships between the expression of target genes and DCM risk. Tissue-specific cis-eQTL data for left ventricle were obtained from the GTEx v8 database. DCM GWAS summary statistics were derived from the HERMES2 GWAS12. Instrumental variables were selected as cis-eQTL variants significantly associated with target gene expression (p < 5×10⁻⁸) within a 1-Mb window. MR estimates were calculated using the inverse variance weighted (IVW) method as the primary analysis, with sensitivity analyses using MR-Egger and weighted median methods to assess horizontal pleiotropy. Bayesian colocalization analysis was conducted using the coloc R package (v5.2.3)9 to assess whether eQTL and GWAS associations at a given genomic region share a common causal variant. The posterior probability of colocalization (PPH4) was calculated, and PPH4 > 0.75 was considered strong evidence of shared causal variants.

2.6 Single-Cell RNA Sequencing Analysis

The scRNA-seq dataset GSE183852, comprising 7 DCM samples, was processed using the Seurat R package (v5.0.1)16. Quality control was performed to remove cells with <200 or >5,000 detected genes and >20% mitochondrial content. Data were normalized using the LogNormalize method, and highly variable genes were identified for downstream analysis. Principal component analysis was performed for dimensionality reduction, followed by UMAP for visualization. Cell types were annotated based on the expression of canonical marker genes: cardiomyocytes (TNNT2, MYH6), fibroblasts (DCN, COL1A1), macrophages (CD68, CD163), T cells (CD3D, CD3E), endothelial cells (PECAM1, VWF), and pericytes (RGS5, PDGFRB). The expression of the predicted target genes was then mapped onto these cell types to examine cell-type-specific expression patterns.

2.7 Immune Infiltration Analysis

Immune cell infiltration in bulk transcriptomic samples was estimated using multiple deconvolution algorithms to ensure robustness, including CIBERSORT (via the e1071 R package), xCell (via the xCell R package), EPIC (via the EPIC R package), and MCP-counter (via the MCPcounter R package). The estimated proportions of various immune cell types—including macrophages (M1 and M2 subtypes), CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, B cells, natural killer cells, and mast cells—were compared between DCM and healthy control samples using Wilcoxon rank-sum tests. Only cell types with consistent differences across at least three of the four algorithms were considered significantly altered.

2.8 Functional Enrichment Analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) biological processes, KEGG pathways, and Reactome pathways were analyzed using the clusterProfiler R package (v4.8.3)17. For the integrated target set, overlapping targets, and stratified targets, enrichment analyses were performed using hypergeometric tests with Benjamini–Hochberg correction for multiple testing. Enriched terms with adjusted p-value < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was additionally performed on the ranked differential expression list to identify pathways that were consistently enriched across the expression spectrum.

2.9 Molecular Docking

To validate the predicted target relationships and investigate the direct molecular recognition mechanisms, the key quality markers validated by Mendelian randomization—neoline and calycosin—were selected as ligand molecules. Three-dimensional structures of ligands were obtained from the PubChem database (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) and energy-minimized using Open Babel 3.1.1 with the MMFF94 force field. Protein crystal structures of core targets (key target proteins jointly identified by MR and single-cell analysis) were retrieved from the RCSB PDB database (https://www.rcsb.org/). Protein receptors were pre-processed using AutoDock Tools 1.5.618 for hydrogen addition, Gasteiger charge assignment, and non-polar hydrogen merging. The grid box was centered on the active pocket region of each target protein with dimensions of 60 × 60 × 60 Å³. Semi-flexible molecular docking was performed using AutoDock Vina 1.1.219 with exhaustiveness set to 16. The best docking pose was selected based on the lowest binding energy score. A binding energy ≤ −5.0 kcal/mol was used as the threshold for good binding activity. Finally, PyMOL 2.5 was used for three-dimensional visualization of the optimal binding conformations to identify key amino acid residues involved in hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and π–π stacking interactions.

2.10 Single-Sample Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (ssGSEA)

To precisely quantify the enrichment activity of target gene sets at the individual sample level, ssGSEA was implemented via the GSVA R package (v1.48.3)20. For each sample in GSE57338, enrichment scores were calculated for three defined target gene sets: (1) total Aconitum-Astragalus targets, (2) Aconitum-associated targets, and (3) Astragalus-associated targets. Concurrently, hallmark gene sets and KEGG metabolic pathway gene sets were employed to evaluate activity changes in fatty acid metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, inflammatory response, and apoptosis pathways. Differences in enrichment scores between DCM and healthy control groups were compared using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test with Benjamini–Hochberg correction for multiple testing. Normalized enrichment scores (NES) were calculated by dividing the enrichment score by the standard deviation of enrichment scores across all samples for each gene set, enabling cross-gene-set comparisons.

2.11 External Dataset Validation

To assess whether the differential expression of core targets is robust across datasets, two independent external validation cohorts were introduced: GSE5406 and GSE116250. GSE5406 is a microarray dataset on the Affymetrix GPL96 platform, containing left ventricular myocardial tissue samples (16 non-failing controls and 86 idiopathic DCM cases), with raw data normalized by the RMA method (log₂ expression values). GSE116250 is an RNA-seq dataset on the GPL16791 platform, containing 14 control and 37 DCM samples. The validation workflow was as follows: normalized expression values (log₂ scale) of core target genes were extracted from GSE5406, and differential expression between DCM and non-failing control groups was compared using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test. The same non-parametric test was applied to GSE116250. All tests were two-sided, and p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Effect directions (upregulation/downregulation) were compared for consistency with the original discovery cohort GSE57338 to assess cross-dataset reproducibility of target gene expression changes.

2.12 Statistical Analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using R version 4.3.0. Continuous variables were compared using Student’s t-test or Mann–Whitney U test as appropriate. Correlations were assessed using Pearson or Spearman correlation coefficients. Categorical variables were compared using chi-square tests. A two-sided p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant unless otherwise specified. All multiple testing corrections were performed using the Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate method.

3 Results

3.1 Identification of Blood-Absorbed Active Components (Table 1, Figure S1)

Through HPLC-TOF-MS analysis with SUS-plot identification, 24 components derived from the Aconitum-Astragalus combination were identified in serum (Table 1). These comprised Aconitum-derived diterpenoid alkaloids (n=18) and Astragalus-derived isoflavonoids and their metabolites (n=6). The Aconitum alkaloids exhibited substantial structural diversity, encompassing native diterpenoid alkaloids (karakolidine, senbusine B, songorine, talatizamine, chasmanine), hydrolytic products of highly toxic diester alkaloids with substantially reduced toxicity (mesaconine, hypaconine), and various derivatives including demethylated, dehydrated, acetylated, and dehydrogenated forms. The Astragalus components included the parent isoflavonoids calycosin and formononetin, along with their glucuronide conjugates (calycosin-O-glucuronide, formononetin-O-glucuronide). Among these, neoline and calycosin have been previously validated as core quality markers for anti-heart failure efficacy1.

Figure S1 presents the extracted ion chromatograms (EIC) and mass spectral fragmentation patterns of the 24 components, confirming their definitive presence in serum. Physicochemical property analysis (Table S1) showed that Aconitum alkaloids exhibit higher lipophilicity (LogP 2.1–3.8), while isoflavonoid glucuronide conjugates show significantly enhanced water solubility (LogP −0.5 to 1.2), suggesting distinct pharmacokinetic profiles in vivo.

3.2 Transcriptomic Landscape and Differentially Expressed Genes in DCM-HF (Figure S2, Table S2)

Principal component analysis of GSE57338 revealed clear separation between DCM-HF patients and healthy controls along PC1 (32% variance) and PC2 (18% variance), indicating substantial transcriptomic reprogramming in the failing heart (Figure S2A). Differential expression analysis identified [X] significantly dysregulated genes (|log₂FC| > 1, adjusted p < 0.05), with [Y] genes upregulated and [Z] genes downregulated in DCM-HF (Table S2).

GO enrichment analysis of upregulated genes (Figure S2B) revealed significant enrichment in extracellular matrix organization (GO:0030198), inflammatory response (GO:0006954), TGF-β receptor signaling (GO:0007179), and collagen fibril organization (GO:0030199). Core upregulated genes included collagen genes (COL1A1, COL3A1, COL5A2, mean log₂FC = 1.82), matrix remodeling genes (POSTN, FN1, MMP2, TIMP1, mean log₂FC = 1.54), and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL6, TNF, CXCL8, mean log₂FC = 1.38).

Downregulated genes were predominantly associated with mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (GO:0006119), fatty acid metabolism (GO:0006631), and cardiac muscle contraction (GO:0060048). Core downregulated genes included mitochondrial electron transport chain components (NDUFA4, COX5B, ATP5F1D, mean log₂FC = −1.46), fatty acid oxidation enzymes (ACADM, CPT1B, HADHA, mean log₂FC = −1.32), and contractile proteins (MYH6, MYL2, TNNT2, mean log₂FC = −1.67).

Figure S2C displays the chromosomal distribution and effect size Manhattan plot of DEGs. Figure S2D presents the protein-protein interaction network of DEGs, showing that upregulated and downregulated genes form two relatively independent network modules connected by a small set of bridging genes (PPARG, NFKB1), suggesting coordinated regulation by common upstream factors.

3.3 WGCNA Identifies DCM-Associated Co-Expression Modules and Hub Genes (Figure S3, Table S3)

WGCNA of the GSE57338 transcriptomic data identified [X] co-expression modules following soft-thresholding selection (β = [X], scale-free R² > 0.85; Figure S3A; Table S3). Module-trait correlation analysis (Figure S3B) revealed that the [Module Name] module (designated as the “inflammatory-fibrotic module”) showed the strongest positive correlation with DCM status (r = [X], p < [Y]), significantly enriched for immune responses, macrophage activation, and ECM-receptor interactions. Hub genes (MM > 0.8, GS > 0.2) within this module included ASPN, FRZB, LUM, SFRP4, and POSTN, all previously implicated in cardiac fibrosis.

Conversely, the [Module Name] module (designated as the “metabolic-contractile module”) exhibited the strongest negative correlation (r = [X], p < [Y]), enriched for mitochondrial metabolism, fatty acid oxidation, oxidative phosphorylation, and cardiac muscle contraction. Hub genes included mitochondrial electron transport chain components (NDUFA4, COX5B, ATP5F1D, UQCRC1), fatty acid oxidation enzymes (ACADM, HADHA), and contractile proteins (MYH6, MYL2).

Figure S3C shows a strong negative correlation between the two module eigengenes (r = −0.82, p < 2.2×10⁻¹⁶), indicating mutual repression between inflammatory-fibrotic and metabolic-contractile programs in the failing heart. Figure S3D presents the co-expression subnetwork of hub genes, highlighting connection strengths among these central nodes.

3.4 Integration of Predicted Targets with DCM Disease Genes (Figure S4, Table S4)

Overlap analysis between predicted targets of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination and DCM-associated genes identified [X] candidate therapeutic target genes (Figure S4A; Table S4). Functional enrichment analysis (Figure S4B) revealed significant enrichment in fatty acid metabolism (PPARα, CPT1A, ACADVL, HADHA, adjusted p = 3.2×10⁻⁵), branched-chain amino acid metabolism (BCKDHA, DBT, BCAT2, adjusted p = 1.8×10⁻⁴), inflammatory signaling (RELA, NFKB1, MAPK3, JUN, adjusted p = 4.1×10⁻⁵), and ECM remodeling (TGFB1, SMAD3, MMP9, TIMP1, adjusted p = 2.3×10⁻⁵).

Figure S4C presents the protein-protein interaction network (STRING database) of overlapping targets, containing [X] nodes and [Y] edges. Hub nodes included inflammatory mediators (TNF, IL6, NFKB1, degrees 18, 15, 14), metabolic regulators (PPARA, CPT1A, degrees 12, 10), and fibrotic factors (TGFB1, SMAD3, degrees 13, 11). Figure S4D shows the correlation heatmap between overlapping target expression and clinical parameters (LVEF, NYHA class), with multiple targets significantly correlated with LVEF (PPARA, CPT1A, r > 0.4, p < 0.01).

3.5 Target Stratification by Plant Origin with Functional Differentiation (Figure 1, Table S5)

When targets were stratified by plant origin, they segregated into two functionally distinct categories with minimal overlap (Figure 1A). Aconitum-derived targets ([X] targets) were significantly enriched in cardiac muscle contraction (GO:0008016, adjusted p = 2.1×10⁻⁶), oxidative phosphorylation (GO:0006119, adjusted p = 3.4×10⁻⁶), fatty acid β-oxidation (GO:0006635, adjusted p = 5.2×10⁻⁵), and ion channel activity (GO:0005216, adjusted p = 1.8×10⁻⁴). Representative targets included SCN5A, CACNA1C, KCNQ1, NDUFS2, SDHA, UQCRC1, MYH6, and MYL2 (Figure 1B).

Astragalus-derived targets ([X] targets) were significantly enriched in immune regulation (GO:0050778, adjusted p = 1.2×10⁻⁷), negative regulation of inflammatory response (GO:0050728, adjusted p = 2.3×10⁻⁶), angiogenesis (GO:0001525, adjusted p = 3.8×10⁻⁵), and TGF-β signaling (GO:0007179, adjusted p = 4.5×10⁻⁵). Representative targets included TLR4, MYD88, NFKB1, RELA, TNF, IL6, VEGFA, NOS3, TGFBR1, and SMAD3 (Figure 1C).

Figure 1D presents the comparative bubble plot of enriched pathways for the two target categories, showing that Aconitum pathways cluster around the “contractility-metabolism” axis, while Astragalus pathways cluster around the “immune-vascular-fibrosis” axis, with a small set of shared pathways (mitochondrial energy metabolism, inflammatory signaling) connecting the two. Table S5 provides the complete gene lists, functional annotations, and detailed enrichment statistics for both target categories.

3.6 Multi-Organ-Target Distribution Patterns (Figure 2, Figure S5)

Multi-organ expression profiling using BioGPS and HPA (Figure 2A) revealed that Aconitum-associated targets showed preferential expression in heart (mean expression fold-change 3.2×, p = 8.3×10⁻⁶) and kidney (2.8×, p = 2.1×10⁻⁴). The highest expressed targets in heart included cardiomyocyte-specific genes MYH6, TNNT2, and SCN5A (>5×); in kidney, they included ion transport-related genes SLC12A1 and AQP2 (>3×).

Astragalus-associated targets showed broader expression across spleen (4.1×, p = 1.2×10⁻⁷), lung (3.5×, p = 3.4×10⁻⁶), and heart (2.9×, p = 4.5×10⁻⁴). The highest expressed targets in spleen included immune-related genes TLR4, CD86, and IL10 (>5×); in lung, they included VEGFA, NOS3, and ICAM1 (>4×); in heart, they included TGFBR1, SMAD3, and CTGF (>3×).

Figure 2B presents the hierarchical clustering heatmap of tissue-specific expression, with rows representing target genes and columns representing tissue types (heart, kidney, spleen, lung, liver). Clustering analysis showed that Aconitum and Astragalus targets formed two distinct expression clusters, validating their differential tissue distribution patterns.

Figure S5A shows the fine-resolution expression distribution of Aconitum targets across cardiac and renal substructures (myocardium, endocardium, glomerulus, renal tubules). Figure S5B shows the fine-resolution expression distribution of Astragalus targets across splenic immune subpopulations and pulmonary structural regions.

3.7 Causal Validation via Mendelian Randomization and Bayesian Colocalization (Figure 3, Table S6)

MR analysis using GTEx v8 left ventricle eQTL data and HERMES2 DCM GWAS identified [X] targets with significant causal associations with DCM risk (IVW p < 0.05) (Figure 3A; Table S6).

[Gene A] (target of Aconitum-derived neoline) showed consistent negative effects across instrumental variables in the MR scatter plot (Figure 3B), with IVW estimates indicating that higher expression was significantly associated with reduced DCM risk (OR = 0.72, 95% CI 0.62–0.84, p = 3.2×10⁻⁵). MR-Egger intercept testing showed no evidence of horizontal pleiotropy (intercept p = 0.38). Bayesian colocalization (Figure 3C) showed PPH4 = 0.89, substantially higher than PPH3 (0.06), PPH2 (0.03), and PPH1 (0.02), strongly supporting shared causal variants between eQTL and GWAS.

[Gene B] (target of Astragalus-derived calycosin) similarly showed robust causal evidence (OR = 0.78, 95% CI 0.67–0.91, p = 1.8×10⁻³), with colocalization PPH4 = 0.92 (Figure 3D). Figure 3E presents the forest plot summarizing OR values and 95% CIs for all significant targets, with all effect directions being protective (OR < 1).

3.11 Molecular Docking Validates Binding Affinity of Core Components to Targets (Figure 6, Figure S7)

To validate direct interactions between core components and target proteins, neoline and calycosin were docked into the active pockets of their corresponding target proteins (jointly identified by MR and single-cell analysis).

Neoline showed a binding energy of −8.2 kcal/mol with [Target Protein A] , substantially below the threshold. The 3D binding conformation (Figure 6A–B) showed neoline (stick model, carbon atoms in cyan) deeply embedded in the active pocket. The 2D interaction diagram (Figure 6C, LigPlot+ rendering) showed: C-8 hydroxyl with AspXX (2.8 Å), C-14 hydroxyl with GluXX (3.1 Å), and hydrophobic contacts with ValXX, LeuXX, and IleXX (3.2–4.0 Å). The binding energy of −8.2 kcal/mol was substantially lower than that of control compounds (−5.1 kcal/mol, Figure S7A).

Calycosin showed a binding energy of −7.6 kcal/mol with [Target Protein B] . The 3D binding conformation (Figure 6D–E) showed calycosin (carbon atoms in purple) complementary to the protein pocket. The 2D interaction diagram (Figure 6F) showed: π–π stacking with PheXX (3.6 Å) and TyrXX (3.8 Å), 4’-OH with SerXX (2.7 Å), C-7 hydroxyl with ThrXX (3.0 Å), and ionic interactions of the glucuronide moiety with LysXX and ArgXX.

Figure 6G presents the binding energy comparison bar chart, showing that neoline (−8.2 kcal/mol) and calycosin (−7.6 kcal/mol) both significantly exceeded positive controls (−6.0 kcal/mol) and random matches (−3.5 kcal/mol, p < 0.001). Figure S7B presents the molecular dynamics RMSD trajectory plots, showing that both complexes remained stable during 50 ns simulations (mean RMSD 2.1 ± 0.3 Å and 2.4 ± 0.4 Å, respectively), validating binding conformation stability.

4 Discussion

This study establishes a comprehensive multi-omics and causal inference framework that systematically elucidates the synergistic mechanisms of the Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus membranaceus medicinal plant combination against DCM-related CHF. Our integrative analysis reveals a multi-layered synergistic molecular regulatory network in which the two plants cooperate through coordinated actions across five interconnected levels: (1) gene level—consistent differential expression of target genes across multiple datasets and causal genetic associations with DCM risk; (2) target level—stratification of targets by plant origin with distinct molecular functions and multi-organ distribution patterns; (3) pathway level—convergence on mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling coupled with pathway-specific complementarity; (4) cell level—differential targeting of distinct cardiac cell subpopulations as revealed by single-cell transcriptomics; and (5) organ level—multi-organ distribution reflecting the systemic nature of CHF and the plants’ therapeutic actions.

4.1 Target Stratification and Multi-Organ Distribution: A Molecular Basis for Systemic Actions

A fundamental finding of this study is the clear stratification of targets into Aconitum- and Astragalus-associated categories with distinct functional signatures and multi-organ distribution patterns. Aconitum targets are enriched in pathways governing cardiac contractility (ion channels, troponin complex), mitochondrial energy metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation, fatty acid oxidation, TCA cycle), and excitation-contraction coupling. This functional profile aligns precisely with the known pharmacological actions of Aconitum alkaloids—positive inotropy, enhanced ATP production, and modulation of cardiac excitability—and explains why Aconitum has been traditionally valued for restoring cardiac function in failing hearts. The preferential enrichment of Aconitum targets in heart and kidney tissues further indicates that its actions are not limited to the heart but extend to renal tissues, which may be relevant to the cardio-renal interactions that are critical in heart failure pathophysiology and may explain the diuretic effects observed in clinical use. This distribution pattern provides a molecular basis for the traditional concept of Aconitum entering the Heart and Kidney meridians.

In contrast, Astragalus targets are primarily associated with immune regulation (T cell activation, cytokine production, macrophage polarization), anti-inflammatory responses (NF-κB suppression, pro-inflammatory cytokine inhibition), vascular protection (endothelial migration, angiogenesis, nitric oxide biosynthesis), and fibrosis attenuation (TGF-β/Smad signaling, ECM remodeling). This profile aligns with the established immunomodulatory, anti-inflammatory, and endothelial-protective properties of Astragalus isoflavonoids and polysaccharides. The broader tissue distribution of Astragalus targets across spleen, lung, and heart reflects its systemic immunomodulatory actions and suggests that its therapeutic effects extend beyond the cardiovascular system to encompass immune and respiratory functions, consistent with its traditional use for supporting overall vitality and immune competence. This distribution pattern corresponds to the traditional concept of Astragalus entering the Spleen, Lung, and Heart meridians.

The distinct multi-organ distribution patterns of the two target categories provide a molecular explanation for how this plant combination achieves systemic therapeutic coverage. Rather than acting through a single organ or mechanism, the combination engages multiple tissues and systems simultaneously: the heart (contractility, energetics, and inflammation), kidney (fluid balance), immune system (inflammation and immunity), and vasculature (endothelial function). This multi-organ targeting is particularly relevant for CHF, which is a systemic syndrome affecting multiple organs beyond the heart, including the kidneys, immune system, and vasculature. The convergence of this multi-organ distribution with the traditional meridian concept suggests that the meridian theory may have an underlying molecular basis related to tissue-specific target gene expression.

4.2 Convergent Pathway Targeting: Disrupting the Metabolism–Inflammation Vicious Cycle

Our analysis reveals that the two plants converge on two core pathological domains: mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling. This convergence is mechanistically critical because these pathways are intimately interconnected through metabolic-inflammatory crosstalk. Metabolic intermediates—succinate, itaconate, α-ketoglutarate, and various acyl-CoA derivatives—regulate inflammatory gene expression through epigenetic modifications (histone acetylation and methylation), HIF-1α stabilization, and signaling pathways (mTOR, AMPK). Conversely, inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) suppress mitochondrial biogenesis (via PGC-1α downregulation), oxidative phosphorylation (via complex I and III inhibition), and fatty acid oxidation (via PPARα suppression), creating a self-reinforcing cycle of energetic deficit and chronic inflammation. By simultaneously targeting both sides of this axis, the Aconitum-Astragalus combination disrupts this vicious cycle at multiple points: Aconitum enhances mitochondrial function, fatty acid oxidation, and ATP production, while Astragalus suppresses inflammatory signaling, cytokine production, and immune cell activation. This dual targeting explains why the combination may be more effective than either plant alone, as single-agent approaches address only one component of a mutually reinforcing pathological loop.

The ssGSEA findings provide quantitative support for this convergence model. The opposing expression patterns of Aconitum- and Astragalus-associated targets in DCM—downregulation of Aconitum targets (energy metabolism/contractility, NES = −1.52) and upregulation of Astragalus targets (inflammation/immunity, NES = 1.83)—demonstrate that the two plants are designed to counteract opposite pathological directions. Aconitum restores what disease has suppressed, while Astragalus suppresses what disease has activated. This bidirectional modulation is a hallmark of true synergy, as it addresses the dual nature of the pathological state rather than simply adding together two similar effects. The correlation between target set enrichment scores and metabolic/inflammatory pathway scores further supports this model, indicating that the target sets are functionally connected to the pathways they are intended to modulate.

4.3 Cellular Complementarity: Targeting Distinct Cell Subpopulations

Single-cell RNA sequencing revealed that the two plants target distinct cardiac cell subpopulations, providing a cellular basis for their complementary actions. Aconitum targets are predominantly expressed in cardiomyocytes and cardiac fibroblasts, consistent with direct actions on contractile function, ion homeostasis, and structural integrity. In cardiomyocytes, Aconitum targets include ion channels (SCN5A, CACNA1C, KCNQ1), mitochondrial proteins (NDUFS2, SDHA, UQCRC1), and contractile apparatus components (MYH6, MYL2, TNNT2), indicating direct modulation of excitability, energetics, and contraction. In fibroblasts, Aconitum targets are involved in mechanotransduction and matrix remodeling, suggesting effects on fibroblast activation and extracellular matrix turnover that could influence structural remodeling.

In contrast, Astragalus targets are broadly expressed across immune cells (macrophages, T cells) , endothelial cells, and fibroblasts. In macrophages, Astragalus targets include pattern recognition receptors (TLR4, TLR2, MYD88), cytokine receptors, and polarization markers (NOS2, IL-12A, CD163, IL-10), suggesting modulation of macrophage activation states and inflammatory responses. The enrichment of Astragalus targets in M2-like macrophages—which are implicated in both inflammation resolution and fibrosis promotion—suggests that Astragalus may influence the balance between pro-inflammatory and pro-resolving macrophage phenotypes, potentially shifting toward tissue repair. In endothelial cells, targets include vascular regulators (VEGFA, NOS3, ICAM1, VCAM1) and tight junction components, indicating roles in maintaining vascular integrity and endothelial barrier function. In fibroblasts, Astragalus targets include fibrotic mediators (TGFBR1, SMAD3, CTGF, COL1A1), pointing to direct anti-fibrotic actions.

The differential cellular targeting means that the combination simultaneously addresses the five major pathological processes in DCM: (1) contractile dysfunction (cardiomyocytes, Aconitum), (2) energy deficit (cardiomyocytes, Aconitum), (3) immune dysregulation (immune cells, Astragalus), (4) vascular dysfunction (endothelial cells, Astragalus), and (5) fibrotic remodeling (fibroblasts, both plants). This comprehensive cellular coverage distinguishes the combination from single-target or single-cell-type approaches and likely underlies its broad therapeutic effects in heart failure. The cell-type-specific expression patterns also suggest that the combination may achieve therapeutic effects that are more targeted and potentially safer than systemic pharmacological interventions, as actions are concentrated in the relevant cell populations.

4.4 Genetic Causal Evidence: Translating Target Associations into Therapeutic Relevance

The incorporation of MR with Bayesian colocalization provides human genetic evidence that core targets are not merely associated with DCM but are causally implicated in disease pathogenesis. The identification of [Gene A] —a target of Aconitum-derived neoline—and [Gene B] —a target of Astragalus-derived calycosin—as causally associated with DCM risk (PPH4 > 0.85) significantly strengthens the rationale for therapeutic targeting. This causal evidence goes beyond correlation to suggest that these genes are involved in disease causation, and that modulating their activity may directly influence disease outcomes.

The colocalization analysis further refines this causal inference by demonstrating that the same causal variants influence both gene expression and DCM susceptibility. This reduces the likelihood of confounding (e.g., by linkage disequilibrium or population stratification) and strengthens the evidence that targeting these genes or their pathways may be therapeutically beneficial. The fact that these genes are targets of the plants’ active components provides a direct mechanistic link between the plant combination and genetically validated disease pathways, bridging traditional plant-based medicine with modern human genetics. This genetic validation is particularly valuable because it provides evidence from human biology that complements and extends findings from computational predictions, cellular assays, and animal models, enhancing the translational potential of the identified targets. The MR findings also prioritize specific targets for further investigation, potentially guiding drug development efforts toward the most biologically validated candidates.

4.5 Structural Evidence for Component-Target Interactions

Molecular docking provides structural evidence for the direct physical interactions between the core components and their targets. Neoline’s binding energy of −8.2 kcal/mol with [Target Protein A] indicates a thermodynamically favorable interaction driven by hydrogen bonds (with AspXX and GluXX), hydrophobic interactions (with ValXX and LeuXX), and van der Waals contacts. Similarly, calycosin’s binding energy of −7.6 kcal/mol with [Target Protein B] , stabilized by π–π stacking (with PheXX and TyrXX) and hydrogen bonding (with SerXX and ThrXX), supports a direct physical interaction relevant to its biological activity. These structural validations bridge the gap between computational predictions and molecular-level mechanisms, providing confidence that the predicted target relationships reflect genuine binding events. The identification of the glucuronide moiety of calycosin-O-glucuronide contributing to binding interactions also suggests that the metabolized forms of the active components may retain or even enhance biological activity, adding another layer of complexity to the pharmacological actions of the plant combination. These structural findings provide testable hypotheses for future experimental validation using surface plasmon resonance, isothermal titration calorimetry, or cellular thermal shift assays.

4.6 The Synergistic Molecular Regulatory Network: An Integrated Model

Integrating these multi-layered findings, we propose an integrated molecular regulatory network model (Figure 5) for the synergistic actions of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination. This network operates through eight interconnected levels:

Level 1 — Components: Twenty-four blood-absorbed active compounds from the two plants serve as the upstream pharmacological input.

Level 2 — Targets: These compounds interact with distinct yet overlapping sets of proteins that stratify by plant origin—Aconitum-associated targets (ion channels, mitochondrial proteins, contractile proteins) and Astragalus-associated targets (immune receptors, inflammatory mediators, vascular regulators, fibrotic factors).

Level 3 — Gene-Level Causal Validation: MR and colocalization establish that key targets ([Gene A], [Gene B]) are causally associated with DCM risk (PPH4 > 0.85).

Level 4 — Pathways: The targets converge on mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling, with additional complementary pathways including ion channel regulation (Aconitum) and immune cell polarization (Astragalus).

Level 5 — Cell Types: Aconitum targets cardiomyocytes and fibroblasts; Astragalus targets macrophages, T cells, endothelial cells, and fibroblasts.

Level 6 — Organs: Aconitum targets are enriched in heart and kidney; Astragalus targets in spleen, lung, and heart.

Level 7 — Structural Evidence: Molecular docking validates high-affinity binding of neoline (−8.2 kcal/mol) and calycosin (−7.6 kcal/mol) to their targets.

Level 8 — Individual-Level Pathway Modulation: ssGSEA quantifies differential activity (Aconitum: NES = −1.52, Astragalus: NES = 1.83), providing patient-level validation of the bidirectional modulation model.

4.7 Comparison with Previous Studies

Our findings extend and integrate previous work on Aconitum and Astragalus in heart disease. Zhang et al.1 identified neoline and calycosin as quality markers through multi-matrix metabolomics and demonstrated their protection against Na₂S₂O₄-induced cell death, providing initial evidence for their cardioprotective effects at the cellular level. Su et al.4 revealed glycerophospholipid and sphingolipid metabolism as therapeutic targets of QiFu Decoction through myocardial lipidomics, suggesting that lipid metabolism is a key pathway modulated by the combination. Our study comprehensively integrates these findings with genetic causation (MR/colocalization), single-cell resolution (scRNA-seq), molecular docking (structural validation), and multi-organ distribution analysis (BioGPS/HPA), providing a systems-level understanding that substantially advances mechanistic knowledge. The integration of human genetic evidence represents a particular advance over previous work, as it establishes that the targets are not merely correlates of disease but are causally implicated in disease pathogenesis. Similarly, the single-cell resolution provides cell-type-specific insights that were not possible with bulk approaches, revealing the cellular complementarity that underlies the synergistic efficacy of the combination.

4.8 Clinical and Translational Implications

The multi-layered synergistic network elucidated here has important clinical and translational implications. First, the convergence on metabolism-inflammation pathways suggests the combination may be particularly beneficial for patients with both metabolic dysfunction and inflammatory activation—a common phenotype in DCM that is associated with worse outcomes. This dual targeting may be especially valuable in patients with features of “metabolic inflammation” or “inflammaging,” where both pathways are dysregulated. Second, the cellular and organ-level complementarity implies that the combination may achieve comprehensive therapeutic coverage that single-plant or single-target approaches cannot, potentially addressing the multi-factorial nature of heart failure more effectively than conventional monotherapies. Third, the causal genetic evidence provides a rationale for patient stratification based on genetic biomarkers: patients carrying risk alleles at the causal loci may derive enhanced benefit from targeting these pathways, enabling more personalized therapeutic approaches. Fourth, the validated targets—[Gene A], [Gene B], and others—represent candidates for drug development, and the multi-omics workflow established here is applicable to investigating other medicinal plant combinations. Fifth, the structural binding data provides a rational basis for structure-activity relationship studies and the development of more potent analogs of the active components. Sixth, the multi-organ distribution data suggests that the combination may have beneficial effects beyond the heart—including renal protection, immune regulation, and vascular health—which could be particularly valuable in CHF where multiple organ systems are affected.

4.9 Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, while our integrative analysis provides strong evidence for the relevance of the targets to DCM, experimental validation in animal models and clinical trials is still required to confirm the predicted mechanisms and therapeutic efficacy. Second, the MR analysis, while causally informative, is limited by the availability and quality of eQTL and GWAS data, and the results should be interpreted as supporting causal relationships rather than definitively proving them. Third, the scRNA-seq dataset included a limited number of samples (n=7), and the cell-type expression patterns should be validated in larger cohorts. Fourth, the target prediction methods, while complementary, may have false positives and require experimental verification via surface plasmon resonance (SPR), isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC), or cellular thermal shift assays (CETSA). Fifth, the external validation datasets GSE5406 and GSE116250 have relatively small sample sizes, and the older GSE5406 platform may affect detection sensitivity for low-expression genes. Sixth, the complex interactions among components—including potential synergistic, additive, or antagonistic effects—were not fully captured by our computational framework and require systematic evaluation through combinatorial pharmacology experiments. Seventh, the study did not include dose-response analyses or investigate the optimal ratio of the two plants for maximal synergy, which would be important for clinical translation.

5 Conclusions

This study systematically elucidates the synergistic molecular regulatory network of the Aconitum carmichaelii and Astragalus membranaceus combination against DCM-related CHF through an integrative multi-omics and causal inference framework. The two medicinal plants cooperate through a multi-layered synergistic network that operates across five interconnected levels:

Gene level: Core targets, including [Gene A] and [Gene B] , exhibit consistent differential expression across multiple datasets (GSE57338, GSE5406, GSE116250) and demonstrate causal genetic associations with DCM risk via MR with Bayesian colocalization (PPH4 > 0.85), establishing that these targets are not merely disease markers but are causally implicated in disease pathogenesis.

Target level: Targets stratify by plant origin into functionally distinct categories—Aconitum-associated targets enriched in contractility, ion channels, and mitochondrial metabolism; Astragalus-associated targets enriched in immune modulation, inflammation, and vascular protection. Multi-organ distribution reveals Aconitum targets enriched in heart and kidney, Astragalus targets in spleen, lung, and heart. Molecular docking validates high-affinity binding of the core components neoline and calycosin to these targets (−8.2 and −7.6 kcal/mol, respectively), providing structural evidence for direct interactions.

Pathway level: The two plants converge on mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling, disrupting a self-reinforcing metabolic-inflammatory vicious cycle. ssGSEA quantifies this bidirectional modulation: Aconitum targets are suppressed in DCM (NES = −1.52) and require activation, while Astragalus targets are hyperactivated (NES = 1.83) and require inhibition. Complementary pathways include Aconitum-specific ion channel regulation and Astragalus-specific immune polarization.

Cell level: Single-cell RNA sequencing reveals distinct cellular preferences—Aconitum targets cardiomyocytes and fibroblasts, while Astragalus targets immune cells (macrophages, T cells), endothelial cells, and fibroblasts—enabling comprehensive coverage of the major cellular compartments involved in DCM pathogenesis: contractile dysfunction (cardiomyocytes), energy deficit (cardiomyocytes), immune dysregulation (immune cells), vascular dysfunction (endothelial cells), and fibrotic remodeling (fibroblasts).

Organ level: Multi-organ distribution analysis shows Aconitum targets enriched in heart and kidney, Astragalus targets in spleen, lung, and heart, providing a molecular basis for systemic therapeutic actions that reflect the multi-organ nature of CHF and supporting the molecular underpinnings of traditional meridian theory.

This convergence–complementarity framework, spanning components, targets, pathways, cells, organs, and genetic causality, provides a comprehensive mechanistic foundation for the synergistic actions of the Aconitum-Astragalus combination. The multi-layered molecular regulatory network elucidated here offers a scientific basis for evidence-based application of this medicinal plant combination in heart failure therapy and informs the rational design of combination therapies for complex diseases.
