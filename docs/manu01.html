Synergistic Mechanisms of Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux and Astragalus membranaceus Combination Against Chronic Heart Failure Due to Dilated Cardiomyopathy: Insights from Multi–Omics and Causal Inference

Authors

Hanzhong Traditional Chinese Medicine Hospital

Xili Wu

Health Science Center, Xi’an Jiaotong University

The Second Affiliated Hospital of Xi’an Jiaotong University

Abstract

The combination of the medicinal plants Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux (ACD) and Astragalus membranaceus (AM) has been an effective centuries–old clinical regimen for dilated cardiomyopathy–induced chronic heart failure (DCM–CHF), while its synergistic mechanisms remain unclear. Here, we integrate network pharmacology, multi omics and causal inference–based bioinformatics, and molecular docking to systematically clarify the synergistic mechanisms of the AC–AM combination against DCM–CHF. Drug and disease targets were systematically identified via public database mining. In total, 24 blood–absorbable active compounds were screened with distinct targeting characteristics. ACD preferentially regulates myocardial contractility and mitochondrial metabolism in cardiomyocytes and fibroblasts, whereas AM modulates inflammation and vascular protection in immune and endothelial cells. Multi–organ distribution analysis demonstrates that AC targets are predominantly enriched in the heart and kidney, while AM targets are concentrated in the spleen, lung, and heart. Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization analyses identified [X] robust causal targets (PPH4 > 0.8). Opposing normalized enrichment score patterns were observed for AC (NES = −1.52) and AM (NES = 1.83). Molecular docking confirmed stable binding of neoline and calycosin to core targets. Consistent target expression trends in external datasets validated result reliability. The results show the AC–AM combination exerts synergistic cardioprotective effects through convergent modulation of mitochondrial energy metabolism and inflammatory signaling. Complementary therapeutic characteristics are observed, with AC specifically improving myocardial contractile performance and AM dominating immune and inflammatory regulation. This work provides a mechanistic basis for the evidence–based clinical application of the ACD–AM medicinal plant combination in the treatment of DCM–induced CHF.

Introduction

Chronic heart failure (CHF) is the terminal common pathological manifestation of various cardiovascular diseases and the leading cause of global cardiovascular mortality. The global prevalence of adult CHF continues to rise in recent years. Affected by genetic variation, environmental incentives and metabolic abnormalities, the incidence of refractory heart failure in young and middle–aged populations has increased year by year. Genetic cardiomyopathy is the primary cause of heart failure, malignant arrhythmia and sudden cardiac death in young and middle–aged individuals. Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), the most common hereditary primary cardiomyopathy, is pathologically characterized by progressive biventricular dilation, ventricular wall thinning, and persistent irreversible decline in myocardial systolic function. Its pathological process involves cascading injuries including cardiomyocyte apoptosis, interstitial fibrosis, immune inflammatory infiltration, and energy metabolism exhaustion. At present, standardized comprehensive intervention systems for DCM–CHF have been established clinically, mainly including neuroendocrine inhibitor drugs such as β–blockers, angiotensin–converting enzyme inhibitors/angiotensin receptor blockers, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, as well as invasive treatments such as ventricular resynchronization device–assisted circulation and end–stage heart transplantation. These interventions can effectively antagonize excessive neuroendocrine activation, improve short–term hemodynamic indicators, relieve clinical symptoms such as chest tightness and fatigue, and reduce the risk of acute malignant cardiovascular events. Despite these pharmacological advances, substantial unmet needs persist, as many patients continue to experience progressive decline in cardiac function, and the underlying pathological mechanisms—encompassing myocardial energy metabolic reprogramming, mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic low–grade inflammation, and maladaptive fibrotic remodeling—remain incompletely addressed by conventional therapeutic approaches.

Medicinal plants have long been utilized as core therapies for heart failure in diverse global traditional medical systems, with widespread application particularly in the countries of East and Southeast Asia [1,2]. Classic medicinal species, such as Astragalus membranaceus [3], Panax ginseng [zhou2026ginsenosides?], Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux [4], Salvia miltiorrhiza [5], Crataegus pinnatifida [6], Ginkgo biloba [1], etc., can effectively alleviate heart failure symptoms and improve patients’ functional capacity and quality of life, with their clinical efficacy well substantiated by long‑term practical experience [1]. Rich in structurally diverse and functionally specific natural small‑molecule compounds, these botanical resources synergistically regulate the intricate pathological networks of heart failure through a multi‑component, multi‑target, and multi‑pathway mode [7]. Benefiting from high safety, low adverse effects, and stable long‑term efficacy, medicinal plants have become pivotal resources for the clinical management of chronic complex diseases and the discovery of novel lead compounds [8]. In recent decades, increasing preclinical and clinical studies have clarified the molecular basis of the cardioprotective effects of medicinal plants. Medicinal plant‑derived bioactive ingredients target core pathogenic signaling cascades of heart failure, such as AMPK‑mediated energy sensing [9], PPAR‑regulated metabolic homeostasis [8], NF‑κB‑induced inflammation [10,11] and TGF‑β/Smad‑mediated myocardial fibrosis[12].

Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux and Astragalus membranaceus are the most classic and widely applied medicinal plants combination in the field of cardiovascular protection, with definite clinical efficacy in adjuvant treatment of heart failure. Modern pharmacological studies have verified that diterpenoid alkaloids from Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux possess significant cardiotonic, positive inotropic, calcium channel regulatory and anti–inflammatory activities, which can directly act on myocardial ion channels to enhance myocardial contractility, improve ventricular diastolic function and inhibit acute myocardial inflammatory injury. Their in vivo hydrolyzed derivatives significantly reduce the cytotoxicity of native alkaloids while retaining core cardioprotective activities, serving as safe and efficient cardiovascular active substances. Astragalus membranaceus is rich in isoflavones and polysaccharides. Isoflavones and their in vivo glucuronide metabolites exhibit excellent antioxidant, anti–fibrotic, endothelial protective and metabolic regulatory activities, which can repair myocardial oxidative stress damage, inhibit abnormal proliferation of cardiac fibroblasts, improve myocardial energy metabolism disorder and maintain vascular endothelial integrity. Notably, these two plants have been shown to act through distinct yet potentially complementary biological pathways, with Aconitum primarily influencing cardiac contractile function and energy metabolism, and Astragalus predominantly modulating inflammatory responses and vascular homeostasis. However, significant gaps remain in understanding the comprehensive mechanisms by which this classic herb pair—Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux (Fuzi) and Astragalus membranaceus (Huangqi)—combats dilated cardiomyopathy‑related chronic heart failure (DCM–CHF). First, the synergistic active ingredients and in vivo metabolic profiles of this botanical combination have not been systematically characterized, hindering a thorough elucidation of its pharmacodynamic material basis. Second, the two herbs exhibit marked differences in molecular targets, tissue distribution, and cell‑type‑specific regulatory properties, adding complexity to the mechanistic basis of their combined action. Third, current pharmacological studies are largely confined to phenotypic observations, making it difficult to distinguish their independent functional modules from their interactive synergistic pathways. Critically, the absence of genetic causal evidence further constrains the modern clinical application and translational development of this classic herb pair. Overall, existing research has yet to systematically unravel the multi‑layered, multi‑scale molecular, cellular, and systemic synergistic mechanisms that underlie the holistic therapeutic efficacy of this combined regimen.

Recent methodological progress in systems pharmacology and multi–omics integration has created unprecedented opportunities to unravel the intricate therapeutic mechanisms of medicinal plants. Network pharmacology has emerged as a powerful tool to predict compound–target interactions and build pharmacological networks that recapitulate the polypharmacological traits of medicinal plants. The rapid development of bioinformatics and genetic epidemiology has provided innovative tools for investigating the mechanisms of complex medicinal plant combinations. Genomics and proteomics profiling further support systematic characterization of the multi–organ distribution patterns of drug targets, mapping the abundance and activation status of target proteins and their encoding genes across cardiac, hepatic, renal and other peripheral tissues, which clarifies tissue–specific pharmacological responsiveness and explains the systemic regulatory characteristics of multi–component herbal formulas. Bulk transcriptomics enables panoramic profiling of dysregulated gene expression in diseased myocardium, whereas single–cell transcriptomics resolves cell–type–specific transcriptional signatures within cardiac tissue, delineating the cellular localization and functional specialization of therapeutic targets. Genome–wide association study (GWAS) datasets identify genetic susceptibility loci for heart diseases, while expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) datasets link genetic variants to aberrant tissue gene expression, enabling deep mechanistic exploration bridging molecular phenotypes and genetic determinants. Built upon these multi–omics resources, causal inference frameworks integrating Mendelian randomization (MR) and Bayesian colocalization leverage genetic variants as instrumental variables to eliminate confounding factors and reverse causality inherent to conventional observational studies. This framework precisely pinpoints core causal targets governing disease progression and substantially elevates the reliability and rigor of investigations into herbal active components and their mechanisms. Collectively, these integrated approaches, merging computational predictions with multi–layered omics data and causal inference, can reveal not only the molecular targets of individual compounds and their distribution signatures across diverse tissues, cell subtypes and signaling pathways but also the synergistic interactions among components that underlie the enhanced efficacy of herbal combinations, thus establishing a novel technical paradigm for modern research on medicinal plant combinations.

In this study, we aim to systematically elucidate the synergistic molecular mechanisms of the combination of Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux and Astragalus membranaceus intervenes in DCM‑CHF, from blood‑absorbed bioactive components, multi‑tissue target distribution, genetic causal mechanisms, cell‑type‑specific regulation, and pathway‑level synergistic modulation. Specifically, we first identified the blood absorbed active ingredients of the combination from public databases and literatures, and predicted their potential targets based public online platforms. Subsequently, we constructed a comprehensive disease related gene set by integrating three sources: (i) DCM HF genetic risk genes derived from GWAS, (ii) high confidence DCM CHF associated genes reported in the literatures, and (iii) transcriptome derived genes, including differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and module genes identified through weighted gene co expression network analysis (WGCNA). We further obtained overlapping genes between the screened drug targets and the integrated disease gene set and performed multi–organ target localization and functional enrichment analyses to systematically characterize the tissue distribution and biological functions of these intersecting target genes. Furthermore, we applied Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization to establish genetic causal links between the core targets and DCM, while integrating single cell RNA sequencing data to finely map the cell type specific expression profiles of the targets and using single sample gene set enrichment analysis (ssGSEA) to quantify inter individual variations in pathway activity. Finally, molecular docking was employed to validate the binding affinities between key bioactive compounds and their target proteins, and the expression trends of the core targets were verified in an independent external cohort. This study, for the first time, identifies the key targets, core regulatory pathways, cell type specific features, and multi organ synergistic patterns of the Aconitum–Astragalus combination against DCM CHF, thereby laying a theoretical basis for its prospective clinical translation in the prevention and treatment of the disease, as well as offering guidance for subsequent mechanistic experiments.

Materials and Methods

Blood–absorbed bioactive compounds retrieval and targets prediction

Blood–absorbed bioactive compounds of Aconitum carmichaelii Debeaux and Astragalus mongholicus were retrieved from the Database of Constituents Absorbed into the Blood and Metabolites of Traditional Chinese Medicine (DCABM–TCM, http://bionet.ncpsb.org.cn/dcabm–tcm/) [13], where the two medicinal materials were searched by inputting their Chinese pinyin “FU ZI” and “HUANG QI” under the “Herb” category (accessed on 15 November 2025). In addition, blood–absorbed constituents of the two medicinal plants identified via HPLC–MS/MS were additionally collected from published peer–reviewed papers. Subsequently, all compound information obtained from DCABM–TCM and supplementary literature was merged, followed by duplicate removal to form a compounds dataset.

Potential targets of the screened medicinal plant–derived compounds were predicted using four authoritative target prediction platforms, including SwissTargetPrediction (https://www.swisstargetprediction.ch/)[14–16], PharmMapper (https://www.lilab–ecust.cn/pharmmapper/) [17–19], SEA (https://seadev.docking.org/)[20] and BATMAN–TCM 2.0 (http://bionet.ncpsb.org.cn/batman–tcm/#/home)[21], with respective screening thresholds: confidence score ≥ 0.7 for SwissTargetPrediction, normalized fit score ≥ 0.7 for PharmMapper, probability score ≥ 0.7 for SEA and confidence score ≥ 0.84 for BATMAN–TCM 2.0 (All databases were accessed on 20 November 2025). All retrieved gene symbols were standardized to HGNC official nomenclature, and duplicate entries were eliminated to establish a high–confidence target dataset of medicinal plant–derived compounds.

DCM–CHF–associated genes identification

A high–confidence, comprehensive target panel underlying DCM–CHF was established through multi–omics integration and literature mining, encompassing bulk myocardial transcriptomic profiles, genome–wide association study (GWAS) data, and published disease–associated literature.

The gene expression dataset GSE57338 (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=57338, accessed on 25 November 2025) was retrieved from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database, from which we exclusively selected left ventricular myocardial biopsies from 136 healthy controls and 82 patients with DCM–CHF. All raw data underwent standard preprocessing pipelines, followed by probe–level filtering to retain only stably detectable transcripts. Differential expression analysis was subsequently performed using the R package limma[22]. Genes with |log₂(fold change)| > 0.585 and adjusted FDR < 0.05 were identified as differentially expressed genes (DEGs).

Weighted gene co–expression network analysis (WGCNA) was conducted on the filtered transcriptomic dataset using the R WGCNA package [23] to screen DCM–CHF–related gene co–expression modules. Hierarchical clustering was applied to eliminate outlier samples. An unsigned topological overlap matrix was built based on pairwise Pearson correlations, and the soft–thresholding power (β) was determined using the pickSoftThreshold function to achieve scale–free network topology approximation. Gene modules were identified by average–linkage hierarchical clustering and dynamic tree–cutting, with a minimum module size of 30 genes and a module merging threshold of 0.25. Modules with module–trait correlation |r| > 0.6 and P < 0.01 for DCM–CHF clinical traits were defined as phenotype–specific key modules. DCM–CHF–related genes in key modules were further screened under the thresholds of module membership (|MM|) > 0.6 and gene significance (|GS|) > 0.2.

GWAS summary statistics of DCM genetic risk loci were retrieved from the HERMES Consortium European-ancestry meta-analysis (HERMES2_GWAS_DCM_EUR, https://cvd.hugeamp.org/dinspector.html?dataset=Henry2024_HFsubtypes_EU, accessed on 25 November 2025), which included 6,001 DCM cases and 449,382 controls[24]. Genome-wide significant variants ((P < 5 1)) were mapped to risk genes positional mapping within ±50 kb of lead variants, supplemented by cardiac tissue eQTL colocalization evidence.

In addition, additional DCM-CHF-associated genes were supplemented through manual curation of authoritative literature. Finally, redundant genes sourced from transcriptomic profiling, GWAS analysis and literature mining were removed, yielding a finalized integrated DCM–CHF–associated genes.

Protein-protein interaction network construction and hub genes identification

Candidate therapeutic targets were obtained by intersecting drug targets of medicinal plants and DCM-CHF disease-related targets. The overlapping target genes were submitted to the STRING v12.0 database (https://string-db.org/, accessed on 28 November 2025) [25] to construct a human protein–protein interaction (PPI) network, with species restricted to Homo sapiens and a confidence score threshold of ≥ 0.7. The resulting PPI network was visualized using Cytoscape v3.10.2 [26], and all isolated nodes were removed to eliminate confounding factors and ensure the reliability of subsequent topological analyses.

Functional module clustering of the refined PPI network was performed using the Molecular Complex Detection (MCODE) algorithm [27], with parameters configured as degree cutoff = 2 and k-core = 2. Maximal Clique Centrality (MCC), Maximum Neighborhood Component (MNC), and Degree, three topological metrics integrated within the cytoHubba [28] plugin of Cytoscape [26], were applied for hub gene ranking. Genes ranked within the top 20 by each of the three metrics were intersected and merged with MCODE-derived module genes. The merged gene set was defined as hub genes for subsequent analysis.

Multi–organ expression profiling of hub genes

Tissue expression profiles of the hub genes across cardiac, renal, lung, splenic, and hepatic tissues were annotated using the BioGPS database (http://biogps.org/, accessed on 28 November 2025) [29–31] and the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) (https://www.proteinatlas.org/, accessed on 28 November 2025) [32,33]. The GeneAtlas U133A (gcrma) dataset (http://biogps.org/dataset/GSE1133/geneatlas-u133a-gcrma/), which profiles 79 human tissue samples using Affymetrix U133A arrays with gcRMA normalization, was utilized for human tissue expression analysis. For HPA-based protein-level profiling, protein expression data were retrieved and processed using the R package HPAanalyze (v1.30.0) [34]. Transcriptomics data within HPA were derived from RNA deep-sequencing (RNA-seq) across normal tissue types. Quantitative tissue expression heatmaps were subsequently generated to compare the tissue enrichment characteristics of these hub genes.

Functional enrichment analysis

Gene Ontology functional enrichment (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analyses were performed using the R package clusterProfiler [35]. The enrichment thresholds were set at P < 0.05 and false discovery rate (FDR) < 0.01.

Myocardial tissue immune infiltration analysis

Transcriptomic profiles of dilated cardiomyopathy with chronic heart failure (DCM-CHF) and normal myocardial samples were analyzed using the R package IOBR (v 2.2.3) [36], which integrates multiple state-of-the-art algorithms for tumor microenvironment deconvolution. Two complementary deconvolution methods, CIBERSORT [37] and MCP-counter [38], were used to evaluate immune cell infiltration. For CIBERSORT analysis, 1,000 permutations were performed, and only samples with a deconvolution P value < 0.05 were retained for subsequent analyses to ensure reliable estimation. Differential immune infiltration between the disease and control groups was assessed using two-tailed Student’s t-tests. Additionally, Pearson correlation analysis was performed to evaluate the associations between the expression levels of core hub genes and the infiltration abundances of critical pathogenic immune cell populations.

Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization

To screen for genes causally affecting dilated cardiomyopathy‑related heart failure (DCM‑CHF), two‑sample Mendelian randomization (MR)[39] was performed by integrating HERMES 2.0 GWAS summary statistics with GTEx v8 (https://gtexportal.org/, accessed on 28 November 2025) heart left ventricular myocardial eQTL data (download date: 25 November 2025). Instrumental variables (IV) were selected at P < 5 × 10^−8, pruned for linkage disequilibrium (LD) (r^2 <0.001 within a 1–Mb window), filtered by F–statistic > 10, and excluded from the MHC region. The inverse–variance weighted (IVW)[40] method with multiplicative random effects was employed as the primary analysis, complemented by MR–Egger[41] and weighted median[42] estimators. Horizontal pleiotropy was assessed via the MR–Egger intercept, heterogeneity via Cochran’s Q, and robustness via leave–one–out and funnel plot inspections.

Bayesian colocalization[43] was subsequently performed using default priors, and a PPH₄ > 0.8 threshold was adopted to identify high confidence shared causal variants, effectively mitigating reverse causality and confounding bias.

Single–cell transcriptomic dissection of core targets

The preprocessed single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) dataset, which contained samples from 2 healthy donors and 5 DCM patients, was downloaded from GEO accession GSE183852 (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE183852, accessed on 25 November 2025). To quantify cell-type-specific transcriptional enrichment of hub gene signatures, AUCell scores were calculated using the R package AUCell (v1.14) [44]. Cell-wise gene expression ranks and corresponding area-under-the-curve (AUC) values of the hub gene panel were generated to represent the relative transcriptional activity of hub genes in individual cells. Expression values of hub genes were subsequently extracted and visualized across all annotated cardiac cell clusters to compare their relative abundance among distinct cardiac cell subtypes.

Single-sample gene set enrichment analysis

Single-sample gene set enrichment analysis (ssGSEA) was implemented using the R package GSVA (v2.6.2)[45], with a custom hub gene list serving as the input gene set. Gene set annotations and gene symbol nomenclature were standardized according to the Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB)[46]. ssGSEA scores were calculated based on log₂-normalized expression data using a weighting exponent of 0.25, with subsequent normalization for gene set size following the default GSVA parameters. Normalized ssGSEA scores were compared across distinct sample groups to identify differentially enriched functional pathways.

Molecular docking

Molecular docking analysis was performed to assess the binding affinity between the key compounds and the hub target proteins. The 3D structures of small-molecule ligands were acquired from the PubChem database (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/, accessed on 15 December 2025)[47] and structurally optimized through energy minimization with the MMFF94 force field. Crystal structures of target proteins were downloaded from the PDB database (https://www.rcsb.org/, accessed on 15 December 2025)[48] and subjected to standardized pretreatment, including water molecule removal and hydrogen atom supplementation, while missing protein residues were repaired using SWISS-MODEL (https://swissmodel.expasy.org/, accessed on 15 December 2025)[49]. Subsequent blind molecular docking was carried out via the CB-Dock2 platform (http://183.56.231.194:8001/cb-dock2/index.php, accessed on 25 December 2025)[50], an integrated tool that combines cavity detection with the AutoDock Vina algorithm for high-precision docking simulation. Key binding parameters including binding free energy (ΔG), hydrogen bond interactions, and steric complementarity were comprehensively analyzed. A threshold of ΔG ≤ −5.0 kcal/mol was defined as the criterion for stable ligand–protein binding.

Independent external transcriptomic dataset validation

The expression trends of core hub genes in two independent external cohorts, GSE5406 (microarray, 86 DCM vs 16 controls, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE5406, accessed on 28 November 2025) and GSE116250 (RNA seq, 37 DCM vs 14 non failing donors, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE116250, accessed on 28 November 2025), were compared with the corresponding trends in the discovery cohort GSE57338.
